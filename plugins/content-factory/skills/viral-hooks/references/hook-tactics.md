# Hook tactics

Small moves worth testing against account baseline. Use these as hypotheses,
not guaranteed ranking tactics or replacements for
a real archetype. A tactic on a flat idea doesn't save it; a tactic
on a strong angle tightens it.

## Specific numbers, only when proven

Precision must come from source, screenshot, log, receipt, or real count.

- "I tested 47 lipsticks" works only if 47 were tested.
- "$413 on coffee gear" works only if receipts support $413.
- Never change 50 into 47 to make copy feel measured.

Rounded numbers can be honest estimates. Label them honestly. False precision is
deception, not hook craft.

Numbers also stretch the on-screen text. "$413" in the corner sells the
hook even when the voice is muted.

## Relevant credibility

A short line establishing relevant credibility may reduce skepticism when
authority is part of the story. Test against a version that starts with pain or proof.

Shape: *"I've spent [time/effort] doing [thing]. Here's what nobody
actually says about it."*

Examples by niche:

- "I've worked in specialty coffee for 8 years. The real upgrade isn't
  the machine."
- "I've shot 200 product videos for skincare brands. The hook everyone
  copies is the wrong one."
- "I've spent six years inside [industry]. The real reason [outcome]
  isn't [common answer]."

Why test it: credibility, curiosity, and payoff expectation can work together.
Account retention and conversion data decide whether ordering helps.

The risk: false authority reads worse than no authority. If you can't
back the claim, don't make it.

## Power verbs and concrete nouns

Weak verbs imply a meeting. Strong verbs imply motion and consequence.

| Reach for | Instead of |
|---|---|
| burned, blew, torched | spent |
| cracked, unlocked-by-fluke | figured out |
| dashed, sprinted | ran |
| smashed, blew past | hit, reached |
| ditched, scrapped | got rid of |
| nailed | got right |

Same with nouns. "Coffee gear" beats "coffee equipment." "$413 lipstick
haul" beats "a few makeup products." Specificity is what makes a hook
sound like a real thing happened, not a script.

## Curiosity gaps with a defined shape

Vague intrigue ("you won't believe what happened") fails because the
brain has no shape to hang the curiosity on. The viewer can't predict
what kind of payoff is coming, so the gap reads as bait.

A well-shaped gap names the category of the answer without giving the
answer:

- "I tried every cold brew maker under $50. One actually works." The
  category (cold brew makers under $50) is named. The answer (which
  one) is held.
- "The expensive serum isn't the problem. It's the order." The
  category (order of products) is named. The fix is implied but not
  shown yet.

A vague gap leaves the viewer wondering whether to care. A shaped gap
leaves the viewer wondering which answer it is.

## Contradiction and pattern interrupts

The brain tends to resist leaving a contradiction unresolved. A line
that breaks an expectation pulls the viewer forward to find out how it
gets reconciled.

Shape: *"Everyone says [X]. Here's why [X] is exactly why [bad outcome]."*

- "Everyone says start with the serum. That's why your skin barrier
  hurts."
- "Everyone says batch your content. That's why every video sounds
  the same."

Pattern interrupts in the visual layer do the same job for the eye: a
freeze frame, an abrupt cut to silence, a sudden zoom, an off-canvas
object that breaks the frame composition. Anything that doesn't match
the rhythm of the surrounding feed.

## Direct callouts to the exact viewer

Naming viewer narrows message and may improve qualified relevance. It can also
reduce broad reach. Choose based on objective.

Shape: *"If you [specific situation], this is for you."*

- "If you've got combination skin and oily T-zone, your toner is the
  problem."
- "If you've posted ten Reels and none broke 1,000 views, this is the
  fix."

When callout is precise, right viewer may recognize relevance quickly. Treat
save-rate effect as experiment, not causal fact.

The failure mode: callouts so broad they apply to no one in particular
("if you want to grow on social media…"). The narrower, the better.

## Cost confession

Burning time or money on the audience's behalf reads as effort, and
visible effort raises perceived value.

Shape: *"I spent [cost] so you don't have to. Here's what I learned."*

- "I spent $400 on coffee gear so you can skip to the $39 one."
- "I read 30 papers on retinol so you can skip to the four that
  matter."

The number does most of the work. A vague "I did a lot of research"
buys nothing. A real receipt buys attention.

## In-media-res openers

Start in the middle of the action. Skip the setup. Explain after.

- Demo videos: open on the result. The viewer sees the after, then the
  voice walks back to the before.
- Process videos: open on the most visually arresting moment, then
  rewind.
- Story videos: open on the punchline of the story, then go back to
  setup.

Works because the visual carries the hook, freeing the verbal line to
do the curiosity gap.

## Staccato pacing when message benefits

Short clauses and tight cuts can help dense openings, but natural delivery may
work better for trust or explanation.

- Two or three short clauses, not one long one.
- Cut connective words. "And," "so," "basically," "honestly" all add
  duration without adding information.
- One visual change inside the hook window (cut, zoom, text pop, or
  b-roll) acts as a micro pattern interrupt and resets the
  habituation curve.

Compare static and cut-heavy versions on same account. Do not assume motion wins.

## Repeat the hook in the on-screen text, compressed

If the verbal hook is "I spent four hundred dollars on coffee gear so
you can skip to the $39 one," the on-screen text isn't the same
sentence. It's "$400 -> $39." Three or four words that sharpen the
spoken line.

Compression rules:

- Drop the verbs.
- Drop the articles.
- Keep the numbers.
- Keep the contrast (the "vs," the arrow, the before/after).

A muted viewer should be able to read the on-screen text and get the
hook in one glance.

## Sound design as timing

A beat drop on the first cut. A record scratch on the contrarian line.
A cut to silence on the reveal. The audio layer is the timing cue that
makes the visual and verbal hooks land at the same moment.

When the audio doesn't match the cut, the hook reads as out of sync,
which is the same as flat to the viewer. Trending sound can work when
it fits the energy of the hook; when it doesn't fit, it's just noise.

## Tactics to layer on a weak draft

When a hook is technically fine but lifeless, try one of these
upgrades in order:

1. Add a proven number when it materially sharpens stake.
2. Add relevant credibility or visible proof when objective needs it.
3. Name the exact viewer.
4. Remove setup words that bury the lede.
5. Swap a generic verb for a power verb.
6. Add a contradiction. State the consensus, then break it.
7. Switch from a statement to a withheld reveal ("I won't say the
   brand, but my [result] is unreal").

If two or three of those upgrades still leave it flat, the angle is
the problem, not the wording. Go back to `references/hook-archetypes.md`
and pick a different starting shape.
