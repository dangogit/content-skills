# Hook archetypes

Named creator frameworks summarized as historical inspiration. Treat them as
starting shapes, not platform laws or verified causal claims.
Mix them. The goal is variety across a batch, not purity within a single
hook.

## Kane Kallaway: the six archetypes

A reusable menu. Any video idea plugs into more than one of these. Drafting
hooks across three or four of these tends to produce better range than
rewording a single idea six times.

1. **Fortuneteller.** "This [thing] is about to change the way [audience]
   [does action]." Stakes a near-future claim and dares the viewer to
   disagree. Strongest when paired with a real demo of the change.
2. **Experimenter.** "I did [X] for [time] to find out [Y]." Buys
   credibility through visible effort. Works well for product tests,
   challenges, and side-by-side comparisons.
3. **Teacher.** "[Person] hit [result] using [unexpected method]."
   Specific outcome, named method, slight novelty in how. Carries advice
   without sounding like a lecture.
4. **Magician.** "Look at this. That's actually a [surprise]." Lets the
   visual carry the hook. The verbal line is a pointer, not the payload.
5. **Investigator.** "I found a [hidden thing] in [familiar place] that
   does [unexpected thing]." Useful when discovery itself is real proof.
6. **Contrarian.** "Everyone thinks [X] because [Y]. They're wrong, and
   here's what's actually happening." Polarizing by design. The payoff has
   to defend the claim or trust collapses.

Test contrasting archetypes against the same account and format. Do not import a
creator's claimed performance ranking without the underlying comparable dataset.

## MrBeast: match the promise, then escalate

The leaked production memo treats the title and the thumbnail as a
contract. The first sentence validates the title. The first shot
validates the thumbnail. If either fails, the viewer feels lied to
and leaves.

Practical rules pulled from the memo:

- Engineer the opening sentence to be a direct echo of the title's
  promise. If the title says "I survived 100 hours in a cave," the
  first line is inside the cave, not in a studio.
- Establish the promised situation promptly, then escalate without unnecessary
  warm-up.
- Inspect the video's retention curve to decide where planning effort belongs.

This is a long-form discipline, but the short-form version is the same
contract on a smaller scale: the first frame matches the first line
matches the on-screen text matches what the video actually delivers.

## Alex Hormozi: hook, retain, reward

Three-stage diagnostic shape.

- **Hook (brief opening beat).** A bold statement, a proven number, or
  a pain-point question. Lead with what hurts or what's at stake for
  the viewer.
- **Retain.** Hold the viewer with concrete content and proof. No
  filler, no setup, no autobiography.
- **Reward.** Close with something usable so they save, share, or
  follow. The reward is the reason the watch wasn't a waste.

Hormozi's title-stem hook formula, lifted from his book on offers:
**"How to [desired result] without [pain point]."** It says the value
in one line and it pre-screens for the right viewer.

Hooks that follow his value equation tend to maximize the dream
outcome and the perceived likelihood, and minimize the time delay
and the effort. A hook is the value equation compressed into a
sentence the viewer believes.

## Russell Brunson and Frank Kern: hook, story, offer

Direct-response shape to test for paid social and identity-led work where the
viewer already understands the problem.

- **Hook.** A pattern interrupt that stops the scroll. Often a bold
  claim, a counterintuitive line, or a visual jolt.
- **Story.** A relatable bridge that ties the viewer's problem to the
  thing being sold.
- **Offer.** The explicit ask.

When an ad underperforms, these are three useful diagnostic areas, not exhaustive causes.
The hook gets the click. The story earns the trust. The offer closes
the loop. On cold organic audiences, this shape can read pitchy in
the first two seconds, so it usually needs softening at the front.

## Dan Koe: APAG (attention, perspective, advantage, gamify)

The Attention stage is the hook. On short-form it lives in opening beat,
first slide, or first on-screen line. Koe's
three filters for whether attention will continue:

- **Relevance.** Does this tie to the viewer's daily life?
- **Resolved pain or potential benefit.** What's actually in it for
  them, in concrete terms?
- **Awareness match.** Is the hook pitched at the viewer's current
  level of problem-awareness, or talking past them?

Tactics he leans on: short opening sentences. Specific numbers only when
they come from real measurement. Explicit pain
callouts so right viewer recognizes relevance quickly.

## Justin Welsh: the three-line hook

Built for feed posts where only the first two or three lines show
before the "see more" cut. Translates cleanly to short-form on-screen
text and verbal cadence.

Format: three short lines, broken by line breaks, ending on a teaser
that earns the click to keep watching.

Reusable template: *"The [relatable enemy] is [negative thing]. The
[hero] is [strong positive]. And I [accelerant]. [Teaser question]?"*

Welsh's most-copied title stem: **"X [lessons / things / mistakes] I
wish I knew before I [did Y]."** It's portable across niches because
the structure carries the hook, not the topic.

## Dickie Bush: big numbers and idea expansion

Two ideas worth stealing.

- **Lead with concrete proven numbers.** "100 hours of footage." "50,000
  subscribers in 90 days." Rounded and exact numbers are both valid when honest.
- **Thirteen expansion vectors per idea.** Tips, stats, steps, stories,
  quotes, benefits, lessons, reasons, mistakes, examples, questions,
  resources, frameworks. The same underlying claim becomes thirteen
  different hooks. When stuck, pick the next vector and rewrite from
  there.

His validation rhythm is also useful: ship many cheap hook attempts
in a low-cost format, watch which ones spark, then expand only the
winners into full videos. Hooks pretest the idea.

## Paddy Galloway: click-to-unpause, packaging plus opener

Two related claims worth applying as one system.

- **Inspect early retention.** Opening drop-off can reveal packaging mismatch,
  but no universal three-second breakout threshold exists.
- **Click-to-unpause.** The thumbnail or first frame depicts a scene
  mid-action, so the viewer's instinct is to play to see what happens
  next. Compose the opening frame as if it has just been paused.

Historical framing: thumbnail and title sell click; opening validates promise.
Exact window depends on format, audience, and video length.

## Choosing the right archetype for an idea

A few shortcuts when matching idea to archetype:

- **Product or result video** with a clear visual payoff: Magician or
  In-media-res (lead with the result frame, explain after).
- **Education or how-to**: Teacher, Hormozi's "How to X without Y," or
  Investigator (you found something the viewer didn't know to look
  for).
- **Strong, defensible opinion**: Contrarian or Fortuneteller. Only
  reach for these if the payoff actually defends the claim.
- **Long, expensive, or visible effort**: Experimenter or Bush's
  big-number lead. The effort is the credibility.
- **Cold audience or paid ad**: Hook-Story-Offer, with the offer
  softened on cold organic.
- **Newsletter or carousel translated to video**: Welsh's three-line
  hook adapted to on-screen text plus voiceover.

When a batch has three or four archetypes represented, the user can
pick the angle that fits the video, not just the wording. That's the
real point of generating across archetypes.
