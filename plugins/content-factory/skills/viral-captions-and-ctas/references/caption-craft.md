# Caption craft

The caption is the layer the algorithm reads as text, and the layer the viewer
reads before deciding to tap. It does two jobs at once: SEO and persuasion.
Optimise for both, in that order, per platform.

## What the caption actually does

Across Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube Shorts, captions feed distribution
through three readable surfaces:

- The written caption itself
- The on-screen text (read by OCR; see `on-screen-text.md`)
- The spoken audio (read by ASR)

The three should reinforce the same topic words. If the video is about
retinol flaking, the caption, the burned-in text, and the voiceover should
all contain "retinol" within the first beat. That repetition is table-stakes
social SEO now, not optional.

## Per-platform shape

### Instagram (Reels and posts)

- Hard cap: 2,200 characters.
- Visible preview varies by client and surface. Put useful topic and tension early.
- Default to concise copy unless story needs more context.
- Treat captions as one search and comprehension surface, not sole ranking signal.

### TikTok

- Hard cap: 4,000 characters.
- Preview and search behavior change. Put useful topic words early and verify
  current product behavior.

### YouTube Shorts

- Verify current title and description limits before publishing.
- Make title accurate and descriptive. Do not claim a fixed visible cutoff.
- Auto-captions are used for search and for auto-translate to 100+
  languages, so a clear voiceover extends international reach for free.

## The first-line rule

The visible portion before the cutoff is a headline. Write it as one. The
rules:

- Lead with the **payoff or the tension**, not with setup or throat-clearing.
- Open a **curiosity gap**: pose a question, a contradiction, or a
  confession the viewer can't resolve without watching or tapping.
- Concrete beats vague. A number, a named thing, a stakes word.
- Prefer shortest line that preserves clear, truthful tension. Test against account data.
- Stacked-hook formulas (visual hook + verbal hook + on-screen text within
  the first 1.5 seconds) tend to lift early retention.

## Keyword placement

- Use the primary keyword **once, early**. Weave in one or two secondary
  terms naturally.
- Do not stuff. Repeating the keyword four or more times reads as low
  quality to both the platform and to Google.
- The Mosseri example: "A guide to easy mid-week pasta recipes for
  beginners" outperforms "Dinner tonight #foodie #yum #recipe" in search,
  because the first contains the actual phrases people type.
- Match the **language your audience uses to search**, not the language a
  category page uses. "Retinol peeling" beats "dermatologic exfoliation
  effects".

## Native vs cross-posted

A caption written for TikTok loses to one rewritten for Reels and another
rewritten for Shorts. The reasons:

- Preview cutoffs and composer behavior differ by platform and device.
- The SEO vocabulary differs by platform. TikTok search behaves more like
  Google than Instagram search does.
- Hashtag conventions differ (see `hashtag-reality.md`).
- The character of the caption shifts: TikTok captions tend to be punchier
  and more meme-aware, Reels captions more reflective, Shorts descriptions
  more SEO-shaped.

Rewrite per platform. Do not copy-paste.

## Caption tone vs voiceover tone

They align on **topic** and **stance**, but they can diverge on **register**:

- The voiceover is performative. Energy, emotion, the bit.
- The caption is reflective. Context, payoff, where to go next.

A mismatch in stance (sarcastic voiceover, earnest caption) confuses the
read and tanks completion. A difference in register is fine and often
welcome.

## When to break the short-caption default

Two cases where a long, slow-build caption can outperform a punchy
one-liner:

1. **Story captions** where the caption itself is the content and the
   video is secondary. Common on text-led Reels and TikTok confessions.
2. **Controversy / opinion captions** where the visible line is
   intentionally vague so the viewer expands to find out what side the
   creator is on.

Both depend on a tight visible hook that earns the expand. If the first
line doesn't pull, the long caption never gets read.

## Anti-patterns to keep out of the caption

- Repeating the on-screen hook verbatim (see `anti-patterns.md`).
- Hashtag walls in place of an actual sentence.
- "Thanks for watching" sign-offs (long-form convention, misfires here).
- Multiple CTAs stacked into one closing line.
- One-emoji "captions" on Instagram, where the caption is the SEO surface.
